Addis Ababa

Ethiopian Airlines operates the busiest hub in Africa. More than 130 destinations connecting the continent to the Middle East, Asia, and Europe. That is a commerce fact before it is an airline fact. Connectivity at that scale reshapes what is possible for any business that moves goods, money, or people through a city.

Addis has four things converging. A metro population approaching 6 million and urbanizing fast. A government that, for all its complexities, is getting infrastructure mostly right — roads, rail, industrial parks. Over 50 public universities built in the last two decades, producing hundreds of thousands of graduates a year. And proximity to the GCC, where a financial and commercial hub is building at historic speed.

The GCC proximity matters more than most people realize. Gulf states are actively looking for trade corridors, investment destinations, and logistics routes into Africa. We have seen this firsthand — Yango deployed $100M into Azerbaijan alone as part of building out the corridor between the Gulf and adjacent markets. Addis sits at that intersection — close enough to serve as a gateway, large enough domestically to justify building for the city itself.

The university pipeline matters for a specific reason. Marketplace businesses need local talent that can operate, manage, and iterate without permanent expat oversight. Cities with strong universities produce that talent. Addis has it.

The infrastructure spend matters because it compresses timelines. A city where the government is building roads and transit is a city where logistics costs fall, not rise. For any business that moves atoms — transport, delivery, commerce — that direction is everything. And as physical AI gets cheaper, cities with infrastructure already being built will integrate autonomous systems faster than cities still debating where to start.

None of this is guaranteed. Ethiopia has real political risk, currency constraints, and a telecom market that only recently opened. But the structural ingredients are there. Ethiopia is crossing the urbanization threshold where agglomeration gains start compounding, and the dividend is concentrating in Addis. Population, education, infrastructure direction, and a geographic position connected to where capital is flowing.

Telecom liberalization is underway. The GCC corridor is forming now. The infrastructure spend is already in the ground. Build early in Addis and you own the density. Wait for consensus and you will find it already belongs to someone else.
