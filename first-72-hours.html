The First 72 Hours in a New City

I get asked a version of this question often: how do you decide if a marketplace can work in a city?

The answer isn't infrastructure. Roads matter, but they're table stakes. The real unlock is what I call marketplace accelerators — the structural conditions that determine whether a two-sided market can reach density fast enough to survive.

The most important accelerator is youth.

Not youth as a demographic talking point. Youth as an adoption engine. Young populations — reflected in the density of universities, nightlife, informal commerce — produce two things marketplaces need: early adopters and bargain chasers. They download the app. They try the discount. They tell their friends. That social loop is the ignition.

But youth alone isn't enough. Brazzaville taught me that.

On paper, Brazzaville looks young. The median age checks out. The universities are there. But the city banned 2- and 3-wheelers. That single regulatory constraint priced the entire young population out of the supply side of transport. The result: high taxi prices, painfully low utilization, and no affordable entry point for a driver to join the marketplace. The demand existed. The supply economics were dead on arrival.

So after youth, I look at constraints. For transport, the critical question is: how do vehicles enter this economy? Import levies, local assembly options, financing availability — these determine whether the unit economics work at the driver level. If a driver can't make the math work on a vehicle, no amount of demand-side subsidies will save you.

Contrast that with Lusaka. Young city, growing fast, no regulatory ban on affordable vehicle types, and import economics that made the unit math work for drivers. All three boxes checked. Within months of launching, the marketplace reached density. Brazzaville and Lusaka look similar on a spreadsheet. On the ground, one had a solvable constraint and the other had a structural wall.

The 72-hour checklist is short. How young is the city — in practice, not on paper? What's the natural constraint on the supply side? Can that constraint be solved with capital, or is it structural?

If the constraint is capital, there's a deal to be made. If it's structural, move on.
