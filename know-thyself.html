Know Thyself

I have been building ventures for 10 years. Three attempts, three different outcomes. ProctorEdu (now Octoproctor) became the largest automated proctoring platform in Europe — profitable since month 6, $20M+ today. PushPullMe failed. Geeklama is early and growing.

The question that runs through all three: how easy it is to misjudge the scale of what you are building and end up with expectations that do not match reality.

The biggest problem of any business — venture-type or not — is finding the niche where you become a near-monopoly. Most companies that win are monopolies, even if they pretend otherwise. Google is a monopoly at search. It positions itself as a tech company, as if it competes with everyone. In search, it competes with no one.

Working in ridehail for the last four to five years taught me this clearly. Geoffrey West found that companies scale sublinearly — they get less innovative per capita as they grow — but cities scale superlinearly. The bigger a city gets, the more productive it becomes per person. A marketplace that achieves density in a city captures that superlinear return. Whoever gets there first wins it all — and density is a city-level question, not a country-level one. Stories of the second ridehail player beating the dominant one almost do not exist. Uber vs Didi in China — once Didi achieved density, there was nothing Uber could do. They spent heavily and still lost. At monopoly level, you have room to defend yourself. Stickiness of users. Business know-how that is difficult to replicate in specific local conditions. I have seen this in city after city.

If we define market density simply as market share, then the place to be is a market with weak or no competition and potential to scale to considerable share fast. TikTok redefined social media by attacking a space with weak competition. Instagram video was weak. Snapchat's content discovery was not great and it was not investing in a global audience. TikTok took those weak points and turned them into dominance. To do this, you cannot be a little better than the competition. You have to be miles ahead. Telegram was miles ahead of Viber. Google was miles ahead of existing search alternatives at launch.

If you do not know what game you are playing, or who you are playing, or where you are playing — building venture scale is nearly impossible.

PushPullMe was a failure because it never built something exceptional for a niche where it could become a monopoly. We provided on-demand counseling for students — a small market with little purchasing power whose alternative was talking to parents or friends. We were not doing anything exceptional. PushPullMe would have been better as a social impact project supporting 500 to 1,000 students sustainably each year.

ProctorEdu was the opposite. It attacked a niche with weak competition and built something miles ahead of the alternatives — including a government-backed competitor. Within a year, largest in Europe by number of conducted tests. Top 3 globally in proctoring algorithms. Raised $300k total, never needed more. The founders now have the flexibility to decide what kind of outcome they want. That is my favorite position for a company to be in.

Geeklama — the jury is still out. We have a thesis on the niche, a product that is magnitudes better than present alternatives, and 40% month-on-month growth executing this strategy.

This also applies to how you capitalize the business. When you take $100k from an angel at a $2M valuation, you are committing to a 10x return — a $20M business. Then you raise another round. And another. Valuations soar, dilution compounds. Until even if you hit the goal, the equity upside goes to someone else. If you are playing the valuations game, you must be in a field with a realistic path to $50M+ scale. If you are not, maybe you should bet on yourself with $20k, build something profitable, and own the outcome.

The question is simple. Do you know what game you are playing?
