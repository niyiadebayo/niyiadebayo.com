Know Thyself

I have been at venture building for 10 years. I have not had a massive win yet. There are decent outcomes — ProctorEdu. There is the failed one — PushPullMe, which consumed the most of my time. And there is Geeklama, still early, still with hope.

The question that has bothered me across all three: how easy it is to miss the scale of what you are building and end up with wildly mismatched expectations of outcome.

The biggest problem of any business — venture-type or not — is finding the niche where you become a near-monopoly. Most companies that win are monopolies, even if they pretend otherwise. Google is a monopoly at search. It positions itself as a tech company, as if it competes with everyone. In search, it competes with no one.

Working in ridehail for the last four to five years taught me this clearly. Whoever achieves market density first wins it all. Stories of the second ridehail player beating the dominant one almost do not exist. Uber vs Didi in China — once Didi achieved density, there was nothing Uber could do. They spent heavily and still lost. At monopoly level, you have room to defend yourself. Stickiness of users. Business know-how that is difficult to replicate in specific local conditions. I have seen this in city after city.

If we define market density simply as market share, then the place to be is a market with weak or no competition and potential to scale to considerable share fast. TikTok redefined social media by attacking a space with weak competition. Instagram video was weak. Snapchat's content discovery was not great and it was not investing in a global audience. TikTok took those weak points and turned them into dominance. To do this, you cannot be a little better than the competition. You have to be miles ahead. Telegram was miles ahead of Viber. Google was miles ahead of existing search alternatives at launch.

If you do not know what game you are playing, or who you are playing, or where you are playing — building venture scale is nearly impossible.

PushPullMe was a failure because it never built something exceptional for a niche where it could become a monopoly. We provided on-demand counseling for students — a small market with little purchasing power whose alternative was talking to parents or friends. We were not doing anything exceptional. PushPullMe would have been better as a social impact project supporting 500 to 1,000 students sustainably each year.

ProctorEdu was the opposite. Within a year, it became the largest automated proctoring solution in Russia by number of conducted tests. Top 3 globally in proctoring algorithms. It won against a government-backed alternative. ProctorEdu raised a total of $300k at a time we did not need it and has not raised since. Profitable since month 6. Today it is at least a $20M venture with flexibility to decide what kind of outcome the founders want. That is my favorite position for a company to be in.

Geeklama — the jury is still out. We have a thesis on the niche, a product that is magnitudes better than present alternatives, and 40% month-on-month growth executing this strategy.

The question is simple. Do you know what game you are playing?
