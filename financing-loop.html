The Financing Loop That Makes Marketplaces Work

In emerging market transport, the asset is the unlock. A car on the road isn't just supply — it's someone's path to economic mobility. The question is how to structure the deal so that the car gets financed, the driver builds equity, and the whole thing doesn't collapse when one link breaks.

The structure that works has four players. Local transport companies acquire vehicles. Drivers lease to own from those companies. Banks provide the financing. And the tech platform — in our case, Yango — provides the data layer that makes the entire loop scoreable.

Here's why the tech platform matters: without it, the bank is lending blind. A transport company with 50 vehicles and a spreadsheet is not a financeable asset class. A transport company with 50 vehicles, each with real-time utilization data, revenue per trip, downtime metrics, and driver performance scores — that's something a credit committee can underwrite.

The more vehicles a transport company acquires and operates on the platform, the more data accumulates, the more financeable they become, and the more capital the bank is willing to deploy. It's a flywheel. Each vehicle makes the next one easier to finance.

But flywheels have failure points.

The two weakest links are the ones furthest from the tech layer: the bank's appetite and the transport company's ground-level operations. The platform can show perfect data. But if the transport company can't stop fuel fraud, can't enforce collection discipline, can't prevent leakages — the margins erode. The bank sees deteriorating portfolio quality and pulls back. The flywheel stalls.

This is why deal architecture in emerging markets isn't just about term sheets. It's about understanding which part of the chain is most likely to break and building guardrails there first. The tech platform solves the information problem. But the operational problem — collections, fuel, maintenance discipline — that requires boots-on-the-ground governance that no dashboard can replace.

We've seen this work in practice. In Lusaka, transport companies operating on the Yango platform scaled from a handful of vehicles to fleets large enough that local banks started competing to finance them. The data made the asset class legible. The operational discipline kept default rates low. The flywheel turned.

Get both right and you have a self-reinforcing system where every vehicle added makes the entire fleet more financeable. Get either wrong and you have Uber Africa circa 2015 — drivers investing their savings into cars with no connected loop to sustain them.
