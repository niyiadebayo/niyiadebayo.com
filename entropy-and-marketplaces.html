Entropy and the Irreversibility of Marketplace Disruption

I studied chemical engineering. The mental model I use most in business is one nobody in tech talks about: entropy and the irreversibility of creative destruction.

In thermodynamics, any real change requires entropy — disorder, energy dissipation, waste. You can't avoid it. But the quality of entropy matters enormously. A reversible process conserves almost all the energy in the system. An irreversible one destroys it permanently.

Marketplaces generate entropy. That's the job. You're rearranging an existing economic system — replacing incumbents, shifting value, rewriting habits. The question isn't whether you'll generate disorder. The question is whether the disorder you create is productive or wasteful.

When Uber launched across African cities, it arrived with a splash. Drivers saw opportunity. Many moved their life savings into buying cars. Demand materialized. For a moment, it looked like disruption was working.

But there was no connected loop. No financing mechanism to renew the asset base. No structure to make the vehicle a financeable, scoreable asset class. The cars that replaced old, inefficient taxis have themselves become old and inefficient — 10+ years into the game. The system generated massive entropy but conserved nothing. The disruption decayed back to the original state.

Compare this to Europe, where Uber's entropy was different but equally destructive. Regulatory pushback in city after city created political entropy that, in some markets, is still destroying potential value a decade later.

The design principle for marketplace disruption is thermodynamic. Generate entropy that is reversible enough to conserve resources — don't burn through driver savings, don't crater regulatory goodwill, don't flood demand into an empty supply pool. But destructive enough to transfer innovation — displace the incumbents, shift the price curve, change consumer behavior permanently.

This is why sequencing matters. When you subsidize demand before supply exists, you create irreversible waste: frustrated customers, burned marketing spend, a brand association with failure. Demand reacts instantly. Supply doesn't. A car needs to be acquired, financed, registered, and put on the road. That takes months, not milliseconds.

Moving fast in emerging markets means building density on the constrained side of the marketplace first. It means generating the right kind of entropy — controlled, directed, and connected to a loop that renews itself.

Anything else is just heat loss.
